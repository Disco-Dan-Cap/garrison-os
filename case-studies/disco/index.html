02 — The short version

The short version

Microsoft and Amazon went public early enough that regular people could ride the curve. Today the Stripes and OpenAIs stay private for a decade-plus — so the upside is gone by the time you're allowed to buy in. Disco opens those private rounds. When a top fund leads a round, Disco's community co-invests on the exact same terms, from $100, and holds the stake as an NFT. I built it because I wanted it to exist — and designed every part of it, from the logo to the last micro-interaction.

03 — How it works

Four steps, no fund admin.

04 — Inside the app

Five tabs, one idea: make private investing feel like something you'd actually open.

The home screen

A feed of live rounds, not a brokerage screen.

The home tab — "Dance Floors" — is a scrollable feed of rounds you can join right now. Each card carries the numbers that matter (raised, valuation, investors) and a funding-progress bar that glows in the round's category color.

It reads like a social feed, not a trading terminal. That was the point: the people locked out of venture aren't intimidated by startups — they're intimidated by the interfaces around them.

Filtering

Filter by sector, stage, or which fund is hosting.

Disco's real value is the fund behind a round, so the filter treats VC Host as a first-class dimension — alongside sector and stage. Sequoia, a16z, Founders Fund, YC.

Selection is the only thing that lights up: an unselected pill is a quiet outline, a selected one goes solid neon-green. Color earns its place by meaning "on," never decoration.

The round

Every round reads like a story and ends in one tap.

Open a round and it's tabbed into Overview, Milestones, and Investors. Type an amount and a live receipt does the math: how many NFT tokens you'll receive, the fee, the network — before you ever commit.

Every number is set in mono and green means money coming in. The whole sheet resolves to a single glowing Invest button.

Wallet

Your portfolio, the way it should look.

One number up top, a glowing performance line, then invested / returns / holdings. The chart emits light because, in this system, an active value is a value that glows.

It's the screen that turns "I own a slice of a startup" from an abstraction into something you check in the morning.

The signature feature

Trade or blend positions — without anyone selling.

The Punch Bowl is a peer-to-peer floor for the NFT positions. Missed a round? Blend a slice of someone else's. Holding one others want? Trade or share it. Smart contracts handle the math; hybrid instruments emerge.

This is the one screen that leans pink and purple — the system's "action / negotiation" register — to set bargaining apart from the calm green of holding.

Social

Founders post to the people who funded them.

Because your cap-table position lives in the app, the companies you backed can talk to you in it. Disco Social is a feed of portfolio-company updates, milestones and press — the part of venture that's usually a quarterly PDF.

Profile

Your track record, charted.

The profile turns a year of investing into a stacked neon bar chart, with each sector keyed to its own color so the mix reads at a glance. ROI, monthly and yearly deltas, and a clean sector breakdown sit underneath.

Charts here are drawn from the system's vocabulary — no off-the-shelf library — so even the analytics feel like the brand.

05 — The system behind the screens

Six rules every screen obeys.

None of those screens are one-offs. They come from a single dark-mode design system I built first — tokens, type, components, charts and a11y — governed by six principles:

Dark-first

Background is always near-black. Cards lift by elevation, not borders.

Glow is the accent

Bars, charts and active icons emit a soft glow. Glow signals "alive."

Mono numbers, always

Every figure renders in JetBrains Mono. A number in a sans face is a bug.

Color is meaning

Green = positive, pink = negative, cyan = link, yellow = warning. Never decoration.

Breathing room

24px card padding, 32px section gaps. Whitespace is the most underused tool.

Agent-native

Every surface has a structured-data twin, authored to be parsed by the app's AI agent.
